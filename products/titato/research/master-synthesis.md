## User pain points (evidence-backed)
- Parents of neurodiverse 3–6 year olds report difficulty finding affordable, consistent between-session support for social skills practice
- Therapy waitlists are long; home practice tools are either too clinical or too generic
- Parents feel unequipped to create structured practice moments in daily life
- Children are often anxious about new social situations (new school, new class, new adult) with no rehearsal mechanism
- Screen time guilt is real — parents want screen time that "leads somewhere," ideally back to real-world interaction

## Unmet needs
- A tool that adapts to what's actually happening in the child's life this week (not generic scenarios)
- Practice that feels like play, not therapy
- Simple parent guidance that doesn't require reading long manuals
- Progress visibility without clinical charts — "are we getting anywhere?"
- Something that works offline, without requiring accounts or data surrender

## Competitive landscape summary
| App | Strength | Gap vs Titato |
|-----|----------|--------------|
| Conversation Skills (iOS) | Conversation practice for ASD | Older age target, no narrative world, no parent context input |
| Social Norms for Autism | SLP-created, customizable | Clinical feel, no character engagement |
| Otsimo | Broad special-ed coverage | Not focused on social communication, no story world |
| Model Me Kids | Social stories with real photos | No interactivity, no avatar, no personalization |
| Social Stories Creator | Parent/educator customization | Tool, not experience — requires significant parent effort |

**Key differentiation:** No current competitor combines (1) a rich character universe from an existing comic IP (Fossil Friends) that children already know, (2) child avatar + role-play, (3) parent-input scenario personalization, and (4) evidence-based scaffolding in a playful non-clinical frame for ages 3–6. The Fossil Friends IP is an independently co-created comic series — this gives Titato a character asset competitors cannot replicate.

## Opportunity areas
1. **Pre-event rehearsal** — parents desperately want a way to prep children for specific upcoming situations. No app does this well.
2. **Engagement through IP** — children form parasocial bonds with characters. Fossil Friends IP gives Titato an asset competitors can't replicate quickly.
3. **Parent confidence** — as important as child engagement. Parents need to feel they're doing something right.
4. **Age gap** — most social skills apps target older children (7+). The 3–6 range is underserved.

## Key assumptions (to be validated)
- Children aged 3–6 will engage with dino characters and follow a simple narrative structure for 5–10 minutes
- Parents will input upcoming events if the UX makes it fast (under 30 seconds)
- Push-to-talk practice moments will be attempted, not skipped
- Therapists and educators will view the app as complementary, not competitive
- Local-only storage is acceptable to early adopters (privacy as feature, not limitation)

## Risks and objections
- Screen time concerns for young children — mitigated by parent-led mode and co-play framing
- "This isn't real therapy" objection — addressed by explicit positioning as home practice companion, not clinical tool
- App Store competition from tic-tac-toe associations with "Titato" name — mitigated by strong subtitle and keyword strategy
- Clinical validation gap — no RCT evidence yet; managed by citing established frameworks (ESDM, Hanen) as design inspiration, not claims of efficacy

## Evidence base summary
| Framework | Core principle | How Titato applies it |
|-----------|---------------|----------------------|
| Early Start Denver Model (ESDM) | Play-based, developmental, joint attention, turn-taking | Session structure: model → imitate → reinforce → generalize |
| Hanen "More Than Words" | Parent-led interaction, everyday routines as practice | Parent-led mode, end-of-session home tips |
| Social Stories | Modeling social understanding, predictable narrative | JSON story scripts with structured social problem → resolution arcs |
| Pivotal Response Training (PRT) | Intrinsic motivation, natural reinforcement | Warm praise tied to effort not outcome; child choice in story navigation |
